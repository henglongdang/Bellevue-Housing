Policy document library

The source trail behind the housing-friction model

This page links the public records and policy materials used to understand why REET exists, how Bellevue uses capital revenue, and how zoning, parking, transportation, and housing plans shape market outcomes.

Source methodology

How documents were evaluated

Documents were evaluated by legal relevance, fiscal relevance, market relevance, and housing-policy relevance. Field materials were scored separately because interviews and observations reveal behavior rather than formal authority.

Source reliability by topic

Legal sources are strongest for statutory authority; field sources are strongest for market behavior. The exhibit uses both because neither alone is enough.

Interpretive rule

Legal documents answer what government may do. Budget documents answer why revenue matters. Housing documents answer what affordability goals exist. Interviews and fieldwork answer how market participants perceive the costs. A rigorous exhibit has to keep those evidence types separate while still comparing them.

The document review supports a wider finding: transfer taxes are one part of an incentive system. Zoning constraints, parking requirements, and capital-funding needs influence whether housing is produced, how expensive it is, and whether sellers list into a slow market.
