About the Researcher

Independent Housing Researcher, Civic Participant, and Future Data Scientist

This project was built to show how direct experience can become structured public analysis.

Background

I am an independent researcher interested in housing policy, civic systems, and data-driven decision making. The project combines public communication, policy reading, field participation, and early data-science thinking.

Motivation

The motivation originated from direct experience preparing a home sale in Bellevue. What began as a personal transaction-cost question became a broader investigation into how local taxes, buyer affordability, seller incentives, and infrastructure funding interact.

Research Activities

  • Interviewed real estate professionals across multiple Eastside specialties.
  • Hosted open houses and recorded visitor concerns.
  • Reviewed policy documents, statutes, budget references, and housing strategy materials.
  • Studied municipal finance and the restrictions attached to capital revenue.
  • Built public-facing educational resources, calculators, dashboards, and research pages.

Skills Demonstrated

The website demonstrates policy analysis, data interpretation, qualitative research, public communication, civic engagement, web development, and data visualization. It also demonstrates an important research habit: refusing to stop at the first explanation when the evidence points to a more complicated system.

Project Impact

Public understanding of housing policy matters because tax decisions are often made through technical language that ordinary residents encounter only when a transaction forces them to care. A digital research exhibit can make those rules visible, connect personal experience to public finance, and invite better civic questions.
