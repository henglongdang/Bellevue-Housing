Open house fieldwork

Buyer language, street conditions, and affordability pressure observed in the field

The open-house work added direct buyer-facing observation to the policy review. Visitors repeatedly moved from features to monthly payment, financing, closing costs, and neighborhood comparison.

Hosting an open house as research

The fieldwork began with practical preparation: property information, basic marketing material, visitor flow, and a simple observation system. I tracked concerns without collecting private financial details. The goal was to hear how buyers naturally framed affordability before being asked a policy question.

The conversations helped separate abstract affordability language from how buyers actually talk. Many visitors did not begin with tax policy. They began with monthly payment, interest rates, family budget, cash needed at closing, and whether a similar home outside Bellevue might leave more room for financing risk.

That distinction matters. If public policy only measures tax rates, it can miss the buyer's lived calculation: payment plus down payment plus closing cash plus maintenance risk plus local cost differences. A local REET can therefore matter even when it is not the first thing a visitor mentions.

Observation method

Redacted, practical, repeatable

Notes were organized by visitor group and topic. Personal names, contact details, and private comments were excluded from the public version. Each topic mention was counted once per visitor group to avoid over-weighting long conversations.

Street-width and pedestrian activity

Built environment as a housing-market variable

The fieldwork also tracked the surrounding environment. Street width, traffic exposure, parking access, and walkability affect how buyers experience a neighborhood before they ever calculate taxes.

Measurement approach

Street-width categories were modeled at 28, 34, 40, and 46 feet. Pedestrian activity was scored from 1 to 5 using observed walking comfort, crossing ease, and visitor comments about neighborhood feel. Traffic exposure was coded low, medium, or high.

Interpretation

The pattern is directionally clear: wider and busier streets reduce perceived pedestrian comfort. That matters for affordability because walkable neighborhoods can reduce transportation dependence, while low-walkability areas push households toward higher car and parking costs.

Fieldwork limitation

Open-house observation captures real buyer language but not final purchase decisions. It cannot prove that a visitor's stated concern caused a transaction outcome. Its strength is behavioral context: it shows which costs buyers noticed and which questions appeared without prompting.
