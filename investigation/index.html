The Investigation

How a Home Sale Became a Civic Research Project

This page presents the project as investigative work: why the question emerged, what questions guided the inquiry, and how evidence was gathered through document review, interviews, open house observation, and policy synthesis.

Section 1

How the Question Emerged

The investigation began during ordinary property sale preparation. I was not originally trying to critique municipal finance. I was trying to understand the cost of selling a home, estimate net proceeds, and make responsible decisions about timing, pricing, and negotiation.

When I built the first cost worksheet, the local Real Estate Excise Tax line stood out because it was tied to the sale price rather than to income, profit, or ability to absorb the cost. That made it feel different from optional improvements or negotiable concessions. It was a rule attached to the transaction itself.

The discovery of local tax variation changed the question. If two sellers are located only a few miles apart but face different local transfer-tax burdens, then municipal boundaries may influence listing decisions. My initial assumption was that the difference was too small to matter. The research challenged that assumption by showing that small percentages can become meaningful when home prices are high, equity is thin, interest rates are elevated, and buyers are already constrained.

The issue appeared worth investigating because it connected personal experience to a public trade-off. Bellevue uses REET revenue for capital purposes, and infrastructure matters. At the same time, housing affordability and market liquidity also matter. A serious civic analysis has to hold both truths at once.

Section 2

Research Questions

Question 1

Does Bellevue's local REET create additional transaction friction?

Question 2

Do sellers incorporate REET costs into listing prices?

Question 3

Does this behavior affect housing affordability?

Question 4

How important is REET revenue for Bellevue infrastructure?

Question 5

Could alternative tax structures achieve similar revenue goals?

Section 3

Research Methodology

The method was designed to move from documents, to field evidence, to synthesis.

Phase 1

Document Review

Read statutes, DOR materials, Bellevue budget references, capital planning documents, and housing policy sources.

Phase 2

Field Interviews

Interviewed seven Eastside real estate professionals using a shared questionnaire and anonymized notes.

Phase 3

Open House Observation

Hosted open houses, interacted with visitors, and logged recurring affordability and financing concerns.

Phase 4

Policy Analysis

Compared the seller-cost theory with public finance needs and infrastructure restrictions.

Phase 5

Synthesis

Translated evidence into public-facing explanations, dashboards, questions, and recommendations.
