Practice

Systems thinking for civic problems

My work is centered on the orchestration of people, data, institutions, and interfaces. I look for the places where a policy rule, a software system, a market behavior, and a human decision meet. That is where complex problems become practical design questions.

This project uses a housing transaction as the entry point, then expands outward into municipal finance, seller behavior, buyer affordability, field observation, and data modeling. The goal is not only to explain a tax. It is to show how a small rule can move through a larger system.

Design orientation

Human-centered intelligence

Human-centered design is not limited to interface polish. In this project, it means asking what sellers see on a closing estimate, what buyers ask during an open house, what policy staff must fund, and what residents need to understand before they can evaluate a civic tradeoff.

The research frames intelligence as an orchestration problem: collect signals from documents, interviews, fieldwork, and models; make the assumptions visible; then present the result in a form people can use.

Research activities

Work completed

The project demonstrates first-hand participation, systems framing, and evidence translation rather than a summary of other people's conclusions.

Interviewed professionals

Structured conversations with seven Eastside real estate professionals, coded by concern level and topic category.

Hosted open house

Observed buyer concerns in a real property setting and reconstructed topic frequency without exposing private details.

Reviewed policy documents

Studied state statutes, Bellevue budget materials, housing strategy sources, zoning concepts, and parking-cost assumptions.

Mapped systems relationships

Connected municipal revenue, transaction behavior, housing supply constraints, and affordability pressure into one research framework.

Built public resources

Created a calculator, document library, visual dashboards, chart system, and research pages.

Developed a data model

Defined a housing-friction index, correlation matrix, ZIP comparison, and future regression framework.

Research ethics

How the project handles evidence

A public-facing project should make its limits visible. This exhibit uses some modeled and reconstructed indicators, so it states assumptions instead of presenting them as official city statistics.

  • Participant privacy: agent identities, offices, clients, and transaction details are protected.
  • Model transparency: assumptions are described in plain language and treated as starting points for future testing.
  • Source separation: legal documents, budget documents, field notes, and modeled indicators are not blended into one evidence category.
  • Human-centered framing: the work asks how a policy is experienced by sellers, buyers, professionals, staff, and residents, not only how it appears in a statute.

Project impact

Why this kind of public research matters

Housing policy often reaches residents through scattered documents, technical language, and cost lines that appear late in the process. A public-facing research exhibit can make those tradeoffs easier to see and easier to question.

The goal is not to reduce infrastructure funding to a slogan. Bellevue needs capital investment. The goal is to ask whether the tools used to raise that money fit the city's affordability and housing-market goals, especially when buyers and sellers are already under pressure.

The project also demonstrates a working method for sociotechnical problems: start with a lived observation, gather evidence across systems, organize the evidence into a model, and communicate the result through narrative and interaction.

Future direction

Evidence-based policy products

The next stage is to turn this research into a structured data project that can test scenarios, compare jurisdictions, and make policy tradeoffs measurable for public use.
