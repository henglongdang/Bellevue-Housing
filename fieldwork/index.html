Hosting an Open House as First-Hand Market Research

The fieldwork page documents direct participation: preparing materials, setting up property information, greeting visitors, recording concerns, and comparing buyer conversations with the policy theory.

Hosting an Open House

Open house work converted the project from desk research into direct field observation. I prepared marketing materials, checked property information, organized sign-in materials, and listened carefully to the questions visitors asked before they decided whether the home felt financially realistic.

The most important lesson was that buyers rarely evaluate a home through a single variable. They combine purchase price, mortgage rate, down payment, monthly payment, neighborhood, schools, commute, HOA dues, taxes, repairs, and uncertainty. That complexity matters because a transfer tax may not be named in every conversation, yet it can still influence the price environment sellers try to preserve.

After each showing, I recorded observations in plain language rather than trying to force every visitor into a numerical category. This made the field log qualitative: it captured concerns, repeated phrases, and the order in which people raised issues.

Reconstructed Field Entries

Observation 01

Visitor asked about monthly payment rather than purchase price. This supported the interview theme that affordability is experienced through cash flow.

Observation 02

Two buyers discussed interest rates before discussing property features. Financing conditions framed the entire visit.

Observation 03

Several visitors expressed concern about affordability, especially the gap between income growth and total housing cost.

Observation 04

Visitors compared Bellevue to neighboring communities, treating municipal location as part of the value calculation.

Observation 05

Financing concerns dominated discussion, which suggested that any added transaction friction matters more when rates are high.
