Full abstract

Housing affordability is shaped by cumulative friction

Housing affordability is frequently discussed through the lens of home prices, interest rates, and housing supply. Far less attention is given to the cumulative effect of transaction frictions: the taxes, fees, regulations, procedural requirements, and market barriers that influence the cost and ease of housing mobility. This study investigates whether seemingly modest transaction-related costs can collectively shape housing affordability, market participation, and residential mobility in high-cost urban environments.

The research originated from a practical observation during the sale of a residential property in Bellevue, Washington. While reviewing closing documents, a municipal Real Estate Excise Tax (REET) of 0.5% emerged as a notable transaction cost. Although relatively small when viewed independently, the tax raised a broader question: at what point do accumulated housing-market frictions begin to influence homeowner behavior, transaction decisions, and overall housing affordability?

To address this question, the study expands beyond the Bellevue REET itself and develops a broader analytical framework termed the Housing Friction Model. This framework conceptualizes housing affordability as the product of interacting constraints rather than a single market variable. Under this model, transaction taxes represent only one component within a larger ecosystem that includes zoning restrictions, parking requirements, permitting processes, mortgage rates, land-use regulations, development costs, and behavioral responses by buyers and sellers.

Using Bellevue as a case study, the project combines policy analysis, municipal financial data, housing-market indicators, public records, stakeholder interviews, open-house observations, and comparative research from peer municipalities. The investigation evaluates how transaction costs influence market outcomes and explores whether these costs operate independently or amplify the effects of other affordability pressures. Particular attention is given to the distinction between direct financial burdens and indirect behavioral consequences, including reduced mobility, delayed transactions, pricing adjustments, and diminished market flexibility.

The analysis finds that the Bellevue REET is unlikely to be a primary driver of housing unaffordability when considered in isolation. Larger structural forces, including regional housing supply constraints, population growth, mortgage interest rates, and land scarcity, exert substantially greater influence on overall housing costs. However, the study suggests that transaction-related costs remain relevant because housing markets are shaped by the accumulation of numerous frictions rather than a single dominant factor. In this context, even relatively modest transaction costs may contribute to broader patterns of reduced mobility and increased barriers to market participation when combined with other existing constraints.

The research further demonstrates that housing policy is often evaluated through isolated interventions despite operating within highly interconnected systems. Policies intended to generate municipal revenue, regulate development, improve infrastructure, or preserve neighborhood character frequently interact in ways that create unintended cumulative burdens. As a result, affordability outcomes may emerge not from any single regulation, tax, or market condition, but from the collective weight of multiple overlapping constraints.

The central thesis of this project is that housing affordability should be understood as a systems problem rather than a single-policy problem. Effective housing policy requires evaluating the cumulative impact of housing-market frictions and recognizing how small costs can interact with larger structural forces. The Bellevue REET serves as a representative case through which broader questions of housing mobility, market efficiency, and affordability can be examined.

Ultimately, this study argues that policymakers should move beyond evaluating individual housing policies in isolation and instead adopt a friction-based framework that measures the combined effects of taxes, regulations, procedural requirements, and market barriers. Such an approach may provide a more accurate understanding of affordability challenges and support the development of policies that preserve public objectives while minimizing unintended obstacles to housing access and mobility.

Study design

The Housing Friction Model

The framework treats affordability as the product of interacting constraints rather than a single market variable.

Case

Bellevue, Washington, with special attention to the 0.5% local Real Estate Excise Tax paid at transfer.

Inputs

Policy analysis, municipal finance records, housing-market indicators, public records, stakeholder interviews, open-house observations, and peer-municipality comparisons.

Behavioral outcomes

Reduced mobility, delayed listings, price adjustments, concession resistance, and diminished market flexibility.

Finding

REET is a contributing friction, not the whole story

Larger structural forces, including housing supply constraints, population growth, mortgage rates, and land scarcity, exert greater influence on overall housing costs. Transaction costs remain relevant because households encounter them inside an already constrained system.

Interpretation limit

The current 0.39 REET-to-days-on-market value is a modeled association for hypothesis testing. It should not be read as proof that REET independently causes longer listings. A stronger causal claim requires a matched control ZIP without local REET, transaction-level data, property controls, and the same observation window.

Policy implication

Evaluate the stack, not just the line item

Housing policy is often reviewed one intervention at a time even though households experience the combined burden. A friction-based framework can help preserve public objectives while reducing unintended barriers to housing access and mobility.
