The investigation

From one seller's closing estimate to a multi-factor housing-friction model

The investigation began with Bellevue's local REET, then expanded into a broader study of transaction costs, zoning constraints, parking assumptions, street design, and market liquidity.

How the question emerged

While preparing to sell residential property in Bellevue, I reviewed the cost side of the transaction with more care than I had before. Agent commissions, title charges, escrow fees, repairs, concessions, and taxes all affected the net proceeds. One local line item stood out because it changed by jurisdiction.

A seller in Bellevue faces a local Real Estate Excise Tax burden of 0.5% of the selling price. At first, half of one percent can sound too small to matter. Applied to a $1.4 million transaction, however, the local amount becomes $7,000 before any other closing-cost pressure is considered.

The research question changed when I put that number inside a real sale environment. A seller does not see REET in isolation. The same net sheet can include staging, repairs, buyer credits, rate-buydown requests, inspection concessions, and uncertainty about how long the home will sit. A small policy cost can become behaviorally important when it appears beside several other costs at once.

The investigation therefore treats Bellevue's REET as an entry point into a larger boundary-cost problem. Municipal lines, zoning rules, parking minimums, street widths, and infrastructure funding rules all shape the economic terms under which homes are built, listed, purchased, and sold.

Research thesis

Housing friction accumulates

The primary hypothesis is that Bellevue's REET is a measurable transaction friction that may amplify market stress when it stacks with larger forces. It is not framed as the primary cause of unaffordability.

Bias safeguard

Because the project began with a seller's own transaction, the research treats that origin as a disclosure rather than evidence. Findings must be supported by public sources, interview coding, field observations, transparent calculator math, and future control-group comparison before they are used for policy claims.

Research design

One hypothesis, five supporting tests

The secondary questions are kept under one thesis: housing affordability should be evaluated through cumulative friction, not a single isolated policy.

Primary hypothesis

When housing prices and borrowing costs are already high, added transaction frictions can influence seller behavior, buyer participation, and mobility even when no single cost explains affordability by itself.

Question 1

Does Bellevue's local REET create additional transaction friction?

Method: calculate local tax burden across sale-price bands and compare it with a no-local-REET scenario.

Question 2

Do sellers incorporate REET costs into listing prices?

Method: code agent interviews for net-proceeds sensitivity, concession resistance, and price-setting language.

Question 3

Does this behavior affect housing affordability?

Method: compare buyer-topic mentions, modeled payment pressure, and estimated cost pass-through.

Question 4

How important is REET revenue for Bellevue infrastructure?

Method: review capital-facility and budget materials to separate revenue purpose from market effect.

Question 5

Could alternative structures achieve similar revenue goals?

Method: build scenario assumptions for thresholds, exemptions, credits, and broader funding mixes.

Quantitative model

The housing-friction index

The index is a structured way to compare unlike policy forces. It does not replace direct legal or economic analysis; it makes the assumptions visible enough to debate.

Formula

Friction Score = 25% x REET burden percentile + 20% x zoning constraint score + 15% x parking cost score + 15% x permit delay score + 15% x mortgage-rate exposure + 10% x street-width walkability penalty.

Each component is normalized to a 0-100 scale. Weighting is intentionally conservative: REET receives the largest single weight because it is the origin of the project, but the model prevents one variable from dominating the full interpretation.

Interpretation

Scores above 70 indicate a policy environment where several cost channels are active at once. Scores from 40 to 70 indicate mixed friction. Scores below 40 indicate either lower transaction exposure or fewer local regulatory burdens. The model is best read comparatively, not as a precise forecast.

Modeled liquidity trend

Days on market are used as a liquidity proxy. As liquidity weakens, the behavioral importance of added closing costs rises.

Statistical approach

What the analysis can and cannot prove

The exhibit uses correlation-style and regression-style interpretations, but it avoids claiming causation from a small observational project.

Correlation analysis

The modeled correlation between REET exposure and days-on-market pressure is 0.39. That suggests moderate association in the research model, not proof that REET independently causes longer listings.

Control benchmark

The next validation step is a matched high-income ZIP in a municipality without local REET over the same time frame. Without that control, the current result remains exploratory.

Trend analysis

The January-June timeline is treated as a market-condition sequence. Rising days-on-market assumptions are paired with interview coding to test whether seller cost sensitivity becomes more visible over time.

Distribution analysis

Parking-cost and visitor-topic distributions are used to show spread, not only averages. This matters because a few high-cost projects can shift feasibility even when the median looks manageable.

Regression-style interpretation

The project reads slope direction and residual risk qualitatively. A larger sample would be needed for formal coefficient estimates, standard errors, and causal identification.

Confidence and limits

Confidence is strongest for transaction arithmetic and source documentation. It is moderate for interview patterns and lower for predictive claims about future market outcomes.

Methodology

Five research phases

The method was deliberately mixed: documents for legal structure, interviews for market behavior, fieldwork for buyer language, and data review for economic context.

Phase 1

Document review

Reviewed state REET statutes, Bellevue REET materials, capital improvement restrictions, budget documents, zoning concepts, parking-cost assumptions, and housing strategy records.

Phase 2

Field interviews

Interviewed seven Eastside real estate professionals while protecting names and client details. Responses were coded by topic, agreement level, and relation to transaction behavior.

Phase 3

Open house observation

Hosted and observed open-house interactions, logging recurring buyer concerns, street/neighborhood comments, and monthly-payment language.

Phase 4

Policy analysis

Compared revenue purpose with market pressure, housing-production incentives, walkability indicators, liquidity concerns, and affordability effects.

Phase 5

Synthesis

Converted findings into public-facing recommendations and a data-science roadmap that can be extended with larger public datasets.
