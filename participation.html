Participation

Interview evidence from market participants closest to the transaction

Seven active Eastside real estate professionals participated in structured conversations about transaction costs, affordability pressure, seller behavior, neighborhood comparisons, and Bellevue's current market conditions.

Interview program

Why interviews mattered

Transaction taxes are legal instruments, but the market impact depends on how people react. Interviews were used to test whether local REET shows up in real pricing conversations and whether sellers treat it as a recoverable cost.

Sampling logic

The interview group was intentionally small and focused. I prioritized active Eastside professionals who regularly hear buyer and seller concerns. The goal was not a statistically representative survey; it was a structured qualitative sample that could reveal repeated market language and help design future quantitative work.

Protection of identities

Names, offices, client details, and transaction-specific information were removed. Participants are described by role because the exhibit is about market patterns, not public attribution.

Coding method

Turning interviews into analyzable evidence

Each interview was coded for topic frequency and concern intensity. Concern intensity used a 1-5 scale: 1 meant the topic was peripheral, 3 meant it affected some decisions, and 5 meant it was repeatedly described as central.

Interview findings

Twenty summarized findings

These findings are phrased as patterns across conversations, not quotes from any single participant.

Finding 1

Most agents reported increased seller sensitivity to closing costs.

Finding 2

Mortgage rates were consistently named as the dominant market factor.

Finding 3

Several sellers tried to preserve a target net-proceeds number.

Finding 4

Taxes were rarely viewed in isolation from commissions, repairs, and concessions.

Finding 5

Transaction costs accumulated across multiple small-looking fees.

Finding 6

Buyers reacted most strongly to monthly payment changes.

Finding 7

Some sellers delayed listing when expected proceeds felt too low.

Finding 8

Boundary comparisons came up when buyers looked across Bellevue, Kirkland, Redmond, and unincorporated areas.

Finding 9

Agents said clear public explanations of REET are hard for clients to find.

Finding 10

Luxury transactions made small percentages feel much larger.

Finding 11

First-time buyers were described as especially vulnerable to payment shock.

Finding 12

Investors evaluated transfer costs against hold periods and expected returns.

Finding 13

Agents saw concessions as a sign of softer buyer demand.

Finding 14

Interviewees generally accepted the need for infrastructure funding.

Finding 15

Several participants favored targeted relief over broad repeal.

Finding 16

Market slowdown made every added cost feel more visible.

Finding 17

Seller education often happened late in the transaction process.

Finding 18

Some agents wanted clearer comparisons across local jurisdictions.

Finding 19

Participants described Bellevue as desirable but increasingly cost-sensitive.

Finding 20

The common recommendation was to study impacts before changing rates.

Limitations

The interview sample is small and intentionally local. It supports disciplined hypothesis-building but cannot estimate population-level opinion by itself. Stronger inference would require a larger survey, transaction-level data, and controls for property type, price band, financing conditions, listing season, and neighborhood.
