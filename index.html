Independent Housing Research • Bellevue, Washington

Is a Small Transaction Tax Variance Creating Housing Market Friction?

A documented civic investigation by a home seller, policy learner, and future data scientist studying how Bellevue real estate excise taxes intersect with affordability, liquidity, and infrastructure finance.

Read the Investigation View Research Dashboard

Bellevue currently layers local REET 1 and REET 2 into a 0.50% local rate. This project uses documented policy sources, anonymized field interviews, and illustrative analysis to ask whether transaction taxes matter more during downturns.

Why I Started This Investigation

I am a home seller.

While preparing to sell residential property in Bellevue, I discovered that transaction costs varied depending on municipal boundaries. A seller in Bellevue could face a different local Real Estate Excise Tax burden than a seller only a few miles away in another jurisdiction or in a policy scenario with a lower local rate.

At first, the difference appeared insignificant. A quarter of one percent does not sound like enough to influence a housing market. It can look like a rounding error next to a seven-figure Eastside home price, a mortgage payoff, brokerage commissions, escrow fees, repairs, staging, and moving costs.

But after speaking with real estate agents, reviewing municipal tax policies, analyzing transaction mathematics, examining local market conditions, and personally hosting open houses, a larger question emerged: what happens when a transaction tax is imposed during a housing downturn?

If sellers attempt to recover those costs through higher listing prices, does the tax ultimately affect affordability, market liquidity, and buyer access? If sellers cannot recover the cost, does it reduce willingness to list, negotiate, or accept lower offers? This website documents that investigation from the perspective of a directly affected resident who wanted to move beyond opinion and build an evidence trail.

Research Activity Summary

The numbers below are an illustrative reconstruction of the research activity behind this civic exhibit.

7 Real Estate Professionals Interviewed

An anonymized interview program captured listing, buyer, broker, investor, construction, and market-analysis viewpoints.

3 Open Houses Hosted

Visitor conversations were logged to understand buyer questions about monthly payments, rates, location, and total costs.

42 Online Research Sources Reviewed

Municipal pages, state statutes, market commentary, and public finance explainers were compared for consistency.

30+ Hours of Policy Review

REET statutes, Bellevue budget materials, comprehensive planning references, and capital facility documents were read.

12 Bellevue REET Documents Examined

Local REET 1, REET 2, capital projects, and budget references were tagged by purpose and revenue use.

8 Washington Statutory References

RCW and DOR materials were used to separate state REET mechanics from local-option authority.

6 NWMLS/Market Materials Analyzed

Market trend notes were treated as context for pricing psychology rather than as single-source proof.

1 Federal Reserve Rate Environment Examined

Mortgage-rate pressure was tracked because agents repeatedly described financing as the dominant buyer constraint.

Interview Insights

  1. Most agents reported increased seller sensitivity to closing costs.
  2. Agents consistently cited mortgage rates as the dominant market factor.
  3. Several agents reported sellers seeking to preserve net proceeds.
  4. Taxes were rarely viewed in isolation; they were discussed as part of a larger closing-cost stack.
  5. Transaction costs accumulated across escrow, title, commissions, repairs, concessions, and REET.
  6. Listing specialists said sellers often calculate backward from a desired net number.
  7. Buyer specialists said monthly payment mattered more than nominal purchase price for many visitors.
  8. Market slowdown risk was described as a negotiation problem before it became a statistical problem.
  9. Agents saw more questions about concessions, buy-downs, and rate relief than about taxes alone.
  10. Tax awareness was moderate because many clients learn the details late in the transaction.
  11. Experienced agents were more likely to mention municipal boundary differences.
  12. Investors were more sensitive to exit costs because taxes directly reduce modeled returns.
  13. Luxury sellers had more flexibility but still resisted visible reductions in net proceeds.
  14. New construction conversations centered on land, financing, and absorption pace, with taxes in the background.
  15. Several agents said small percentages feel larger when equity is thin or prices soften.
  16. Professionals warned against blaming one tax for the entire affordability crisis.
  17. The strongest consensus was that rate pressure magnifies every other cost.
  18. Agents supported clearer public education about who pays REET and how proceeds are used.
  19. Some participants favored flexible or tiered structures if revenue stability could be protected.
  20. The interviews shifted the project from a complaint about a fee to a broader inquiry into liquidity, infrastructure, and fairness.
