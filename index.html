Executive summary

Housing affordability is the cumulative outcome of interacting market frictions

This project tests one main claim: Bellevue's 0.5% local REET is unlikely to explain housing affordability by itself, but it can add measurable friction when a household is already facing high prices, mortgage-rate pressure, concessions, repair risk, and limited housing choices.

Taxes, regulations, permitting processes, financing conditions, and development constraints collectively shape affordability and housing mobility.
Direct cost

The rate becomes cash at closing

At Bellevue price levels, 0.5% is not abstract. On a $1.4 million sale, the local amount is $7,000 before commissions, escrow, title, repairs, concessions, or moving costs are counted.

Behavioral test

Net-proceeds pressure can change choices

The research asks whether sellers delay listing, resist concessions, or set prices differently when added local costs appear beside a weaker buyer pool and higher monthly-payment pressure.

Policy frame

Revenue purpose and market effect both matter

Bellevue needs capital funding. The policy question is whether the revenue design can be evaluated against mobility, liquidity, affordability, and market-cycle conditions without treating repeal as the only option.

Discovery

From a closing estimate to a system map

Practical observation

The research originated from a practical observation during the sale of a residential property in Bellevue, Washington. While reviewing closing documents, a municipal Real Estate Excise Tax (REET) of 0.5% emerged as a notable transaction cost.

Broader question

Although relatively small when viewed independently, the tax raised a broader question: at what point do accumulated housing-market frictions begin to influence homeowner behavior, transaction decisions, and overall housing affordability?

Research direction

To address this question, the study expands beyond the Bellevue REET itself and develops a broader analytical framework termed the Housing Friction Model.

Analysis

The Housing Friction Model as the centerpiece

This framework conceptualizes housing affordability as the product of interacting constraints rather than a single market variable. Under this model, transaction taxes represent only one component within a larger ecosystem that includes zoning restrictions, parking requirements, permitting processes, mortgage rates, land-use regulations, development costs, and behavioral responses by buyers and sellers.

Friction Score formula

Friction Score = 25% x REET burden percentile + 20% x zoning constraint score + 15% x parking cost score + 15% x permit delay score + 15% x mortgage-rate exposure + 10% x street-width walkability penalty.

Each component is normalized to a 0-100 scale. Weighting is intentionally conservative: REET receives the largest single weight because it is the origin of the project, but the model prevents one variable from dominating the full interpretation.

Findings

What the analysis suggests

The analysis finds that the Bellevue REET is unlikely to be a primary driver of housing unaffordability when considered in isolation. Larger structural forces, including regional housing supply constraints, population growth, mortgage interest rates, and land scarcity, exert substantially greater influence on overall housing costs.

Finding

REET is not the whole story

However, the study suggests that transaction-related costs remain relevant because housing markets are shaped by the accumulation of numerous frictions rather than a single dominant factor.

Finding

Small costs interact with larger forces

In this context, even relatively modest transaction costs may contribute to broader patterns of reduced mobility and increased barriers to market participation when combined with other existing constraints.

Finding

Policy is evaluated too narrowly

The research further demonstrates that housing policy is often evaluated through isolated interventions despite operating within highly interconnected systems.

Finding

Burden is cumulative

Policies intended to generate municipal revenue, regulate development, improve infrastructure, or preserve neighborhood character frequently interact in ways that create unintended cumulative burdens.

Finding

Affordability outcomes are systemic

As a result, affordability outcomes may emerge not from any single regulation, tax, or market condition, but from the collective weight of multiple overlapping constraints.

Finding

Control-group testing is the next standard

The next validation step is a matched high-income ZIP in a municipality without local REET over the same time frame. Without that control, the current result remains exploratory.

Policy implications

Keep infrastructure funding, reduce housing friction

The research points toward practical policy review instead of simple repeal. Bellevue can preserve capital revenue while asking whether the tax design should respond to affordability, liquidity, and market-cycle conditions.

Publish clearer seller guidance

Closing-cost examples should be easy to find, plain-spoken, and tied to realistic Bellevue price points. The public should be able to see how Bellevue's 0.5% local REET translates into dollar burdens across common sale-price bands.

Study market-cycle sensitivity

Bellevue should evaluate how transfer-tax revenue behaves when listings slow and mortgage rates rise. A policy that looks stable in an expansion can behave differently when inventory, days on market, and buyer budgets tighten together.

Model alternatives

Policy staff could test exemptions, thresholds, credits, parking reforms, and broader funding mixes against capital-plan needs. The goal is to compare revenue stability with housing-production incentives, not to treat one tax line as the whole system.

Conclusion

Evaluate the stack, not just the line item

The central thesis of this project is that housing affordability should be understood as a systems problem rather than a single-policy problem. Effective housing policy requires evaluating the cumulative impact of housing-market frictions and recognizing how small costs can interact with larger structural forces.

Final argument

Ultimately, this study argues that policymakers should move beyond evaluating individual housing policies in isolation and instead adopt a friction-based framework that measures the combined effects of taxes, regulations, procedural requirements, and market barriers. Such an approach may provide a more accurate understanding of affordability challenges and support the development of policies that preserve public objectives while minimizing unintended obstacles to housing access and mobility.
