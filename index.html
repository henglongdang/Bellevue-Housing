Policy problem

Transfer taxes are small in rate form and large in transaction form

Bellevue's local Real Estate Excise Tax is measured as a percentage, but sellers experience it as a dollar amount at closing. This exhibit keeps the original policy explainer in place and expands it into a broader empirical question: when housing is already expensive, how much friction can the local system add before households change behavior?

The tax point

REET is paid at transfer

The cost appears during closing. That makes it different from a broad annual tax because it concentrates the burden on households and owners who are moving, downsizing, settling estates, relocating for work, or selling investment property.

The market concern

Sellers protect net proceeds

When a seller sees a higher closing-cost estimate, the natural response is to preserve the net number. In a soft market, that can lead to delayed listings, higher initial asking prices, or resistance to buyer concessions.

The civic tradeoff

Bellevue needs capital funding

The question is not whether infrastructure matters. It does. The question is whether the revenue tool should become more sensitive to market conditions, housing supply, and affordability pressure.

Seller cost model

Small percentages become visible at Bellevue prices

Half of one percent can look modest until it is applied to a seven-figure transaction. The calculator keeps the original site function in place and adds a research interpretation: transfer costs matter most when sellers are already negotiating repairs, concessions, rate buydowns, and timing risk.

Working assumption

This tool is educational. It uses a simplified state estimate and local-rate comparison so visitors can see how municipal boundaries change transaction costs. A closing agent or tax professional should confirm any live transaction.

Why I started looking

The issue began with a real sale decision

I was preparing to sell residential property in Bellevue and noticed that local transaction costs were not the same on both sides of nearby municipal boundaries. What first looked like a small line item became a broader research question about market behavior during a downturn.

Personal stake

I approached the issue as a seller affected by closing costs, not as a campaign staffer or paid consultant. That perspective shaped the research: start with the transaction math, then test the assumptions against agents, buyers, policy documents, field observations, and modeled data.

Research direction

The investigation asked whether sellers try to recover transaction costs through price, whether buyers notice the combined affordability pressure, and whether Bellevue can keep important capital revenue while reducing friction when the housing market slows.

Quantitative frame

The analysis treats REET as one measurable component inside a larger housing-friction index. The index combines local transfer cost, estimated parking-cost burden, zoning constraint intensity, street-width walkability effects, permit-delay risk, and mortgage-rate exposure. Scores are normalized to a 0-100 scale so indicators with different units can be compared without claiming false precision.

Affordability pressure stack

Civic recommendations

Keep infrastructure funding, reduce housing friction

The research points toward practical policy review instead of simple repeal. Bellevue can preserve capital revenue while asking whether the tax design should respond to affordability, liquidity, and market-cycle conditions.

Publish clearer seller guidance

Closing-cost examples should be easy to find, plain-spoken, and tied to realistic Bellevue price points. The public should be able to see how Bellevue's 0.5% local REET translates into dollar burdens across common sale-price bands.

Study market-cycle sensitivity

Bellevue should evaluate how transfer-tax revenue behaves when listings slow and mortgage rates rise. A policy that looks stable in an expansion can behave differently when inventory, days on market, and buyer budgets tighten together.

Model alternatives

Policy staff could test exemptions, thresholds, credits, parking reforms, and broader funding mixes against capital-plan needs. The goal is to compare revenue stability with housing-production incentives, not to treat one tax line as the whole system.
